Topic-wise Weightage Analysis

Why Weightage Analysis Matters

Weightage Analysis is the systematic study of how marks are distributed across topics in previous GATE DA papers. It reveals which topics are consistently high-yield and which ones appear rarely, allowing you to allocate study time proportionally.

Studying every topic equally is inefficient. The Pareto principle applies here: roughly 80% of the marks come from about 40–50% of the syllabus. By identifying and mastering the high-weightage topics, you can maximize your score with focused effort.

Remember: Weightage analysis is a guide, not a guarantee. GATE can always introduce surprise questions from less-common topics. Use this as a prioritization tool, not an excuse to skip topics entirely.

High-Priority Topics (Must Master)

These topics alone can account for 18–23 marks out of 30 in the AI section. Mastering them is non-negotiable for a competitive score.

1. Machine Learning — Supervised Learning

Expected marks: 5–8 marks per paper
Key sub-topics:
  • Linear Regression (closed-form solution, gradient descent, regularization)
  • Logistic Regression (sigmoid, cross-entropy loss, decision boundary)
  • Support Vector Machines (margin, kernel trick, soft margin)
  • Decision Trees (entropy, information gain, Gini index, pruning)
  • Bias-Variance Tradeoff
  • Evaluation Metrics (accuracy, precision, recall, F1, ROC-AUC)
  • Cross-validation techniques

2. Machine Learning — Unsupervised Learning

Expected marks: 2–4 marks per paper
Key sub-topics:
  • K-Means Clustering (algorithm, convergence, choosing K)
  • Hierarchical Clustering (agglomerative, dendrograms)
  • Principal Component Analysis (eigenvalue decomposition, variance explained)
  • Dimensionality Reduction concepts

3. Search Algorithms

Expected marks: 4–6 marks per paper
Key sub-topics:
  • BFS, DFS, Uniform Cost Search (complexity, completeness, optimality)
  • A* Search (admissibility, consistency, optimality proofs)
  • Greedy Best-First Search
  • Heuristic design and admissibility verification
  • State space representation and problem formulation
  • Iterative Deepening DFS

4. Probabilistic Reasoning

Expected marks: 3–5 marks per paper
Key sub-topics:
  • Bayes’ Theorem and its applications
  • Bayesian Networks (conditional independence, d-separation)
  • Naive Bayes Classifier
  • Joint and conditional probability calculations
  • Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE)

Medium-Priority Topics (Should Know Well)

These topics appear regularly but with fewer marks. Solid understanding is needed, but exhaustive depth may not be required.

5. Neural Networks & Deep Learning

Expected marks: 3–5 marks per paper
Key sub-topics:
  • Perceptron & Multi-Layer Perceptron
  • Backpropagation algorithm (chain rule, weight updates)
  • Activation functions (sigmoid, ReLU, tanh, softmax)
  • Convolutional Neural Networks (convolution, pooling, feature maps)
  • Recurrent Neural Networks (basic architecture, vanishing gradient)
  • Regularization (dropout, batch normalization, early stopping)

6. Logic (Propositional & Predicate)

Expected marks: 2–4 marks per paper
Key sub-topics:
  • Truth tables and logical equivalences
  • CNF and DNF conversions
  • Resolution and inference rules
  • First-Order Logic: quantifiers, unification
  • Modus Ponens, Modus Tollens
  • Validity, satisfiability, and entailment

7. Adversarial Search

Expected marks: 2–3 marks per paper
Key sub-topics:
  • Minimax algorithm (full tree evaluation)
  • Alpha-Beta Pruning (node elimination, move ordering)
  • Game tree construction
  • Expectiminimax for stochastic games

8. NLP Fundamentals

Expected marks: 1–3 marks per paper
Key sub-topics:
  • Text preprocessing (tokenization, stemming, lemmatization)
  • Bag of Words, TF-IDF
  • Word embeddings (Word2Vec concepts)
  • N-gram language models
  • Basic sentiment analysis concepts

Low-Priority Topics (Good to Know)

These topics appear sporadically. A conceptual understanding is usually sufficient; deep problem-solving practice may not be needed.

Topics in this category:
  • Reinforcement Learning: MDPs, value iteration, policy iteration, Q-learning basics (1–2 marks)
  • Planning: STRIPS representation, classical planning, partial-order planning (0–2 marks)
  • Constraint Satisfaction: CSP formulation, backtracking, arc consistency (0–2 marks)
  • Computer Vision: Image fundamentals, convolution operations, edge detection (0–1 marks)
  • Advanced Topics: Explainable AI, ethics in AI, multi-agent systems (0–1 marks)
Don’t skip low-priority topics entirely! In some years, a 2-mark question from a “low-priority” topic can appear. Having basic conceptual clarity ensures you can attempt it correctly and gain those marks.

Optimal Study Plan Based on Weightage

Based on the weightage analysis, here is a recommended study order that maximizes marks gained per unit of study time:

Phase 1: High-ROI Topics (First 60% of study time)
  1. Machine Learning Foundations (Ch 08) — Spend the most time here. This single chapter can yield 8–12 marks.
  2. Reasoning Under Uncertainty (Ch 07) — Bayes’ theorem and probabilistic reasoning are tested every year.
  3. Search Algorithms (Ch 02 + Ch 03) — Consistently 4–6 marks. Algorithms are procedural and easy to master with practice.
  4. Neural Networks (Ch 09) — Growing in importance. Backpropagation and CNN concepts are frequently tested.
Phase 2: Medium-ROI Topics (Next 25% of study time)
  1. Adversarial Search (Ch 04) — Minimax and Alpha-Beta are predictable question patterns.
  2. Logic (Ch 05 + Ch 06) — Important for 2–4 marks. Focus on resolution and inference.
  3. NLP (Ch 10) — Increasing trend. Basics are usually sufficient.
Phase 3: Low-ROI Topics (Last 15% of study time)
  1. Reinforcement Learning (Ch 12) — Focus on MDPs and Bellman equations only.
  2. CSP (Ch 14) — Basic formulation and backtracking.
  3. Planning (Ch 13), Computer Vision (Ch 11), Advanced Topics (Ch 15) — Conceptual awareness only.

Strategic Recommendations

Summary of Key Strategies:
  1. Allocate 60% of study time to ML + Probability + Search + Neural Networks. These four clusters account for ~20 out of 30 AI marks.
  2. Practice numerical problems extensively. The majority of high-weightage topics are tested via NAT questions requiring precise computation.
  3. Don’t neglect Logic. It’s a reliable 2–4 marks every year and is conceptually distinct from other topics.
  4. Track the trend. Deep Learning and NLP are gaining weightage. Expect more questions on CNNs, RNNs, and word embeddings in upcoming years.
  5. Minimum viable coverage for low-priority topics. For RL, Planning, and CSP, know the basics well enough to attempt any straightforward question that appears.
  6. Solve previous year questions topic-wise. This is the most reliable way to calibrate your preparation against actual exam expectations.
