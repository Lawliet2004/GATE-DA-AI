GATE DA Exam Pattern & Strategy

Exam Overview

GATE DA (Data Science and Artificial Intelligence) is one of the 30 papers offered in the Graduate Aptitude Test in Engineering. It was introduced in 2024 and tests candidates on core areas including Probability & Statistics, Linear Algebra, Machine Learning, AI, and Programming & Data Structures.

The exam is conducted once a year, typically in February, by one of the IITs on a rotational basis. It is a 3-hour computer-based test (CBT) administered online at designated centres across India and select international locations.

Key facts at a glance:

  • Duration: 3 hours (180 minutes)
  • Total marks: 100
  • Total questions: 65
  • Question types: MCQ, MSQ, and NAT
  • No sectional time limits — you can switch freely between sections
  • Virtual scientific calculator available on-screen

Question Types Explained

Understanding the three question formats is the first step to building the right exam strategy.

Multiple Choice Questions (MCQ)

Format: A question with exactly one correct answer out of four options (A, B, C, D). You must select one option. Negative marking applies for wrong answers.

MCQs form the majority of the paper. They test both conceptual understanding and quick calculation. Since negative marking applies, avoid blind guessing — but educated guesses (eliminating 2+ options) are statistically beneficial.

Multiple Select Questions (MSQ)

Format: A question with one or more correct answers out of four options. You must select all correct options to receive marks. There is no partial marking and no negative marking.

MSQs are often considered trickier because you must identify every correct answer. However, the absence of negative marking makes them safer to attempt. Even if unsure, attempting MSQs costs nothing.

Numerical Answer Type (NAT)

Format: You type a numerical value (integer or decimal) using the on-screen keyboard. There are no options to choose from, no negative marking, and an acceptable range is used for evaluation.

NAT questions reward precise calculation skills. The acceptable range is usually announced per question. Always double-check your arithmetic and round off correctly.

Marking Scheme

The GATE DA marking scheme varies by question type and mark value. Below is the complete breakdown:

Question Type Marks Correct Answer Wrong Answer Unattempted
MCQ 1 mark +1 −1/3 0
MCQ 2 marks +2 −2/3 0
MSQ 1 or 2 marks +1 or +2 0 0
NAT 1 or 2 marks +1 or +2 0 0
Key Insight: Negative marking applies only to MCQs. MSQ and NAT have zero penalty. This means you should always attempt every MSQ and NAT question, even if you have to guess.

Section Breakdown

GATE DA is divided into three sections. Understanding the weightage of each section helps in prioritising your preparation.

Section 1: General Aptitude (GA)

Marks: 15 out of 100
Questions: 10 (5 × 1-mark + 5 × 2-mark)
Topics: Verbal Ability (English grammar, vocabulary, reading comprehension) and Quantitative Aptitude (basic mathematics, data interpretation, reasoning)

GA is common across all GATE papers. It is generally considered easier and provides “free marks” if prepared well. Spending 15–20 minutes here is usually sufficient.

Section 2: Core Subjects

Marks: 55 out of 100
Topics: Probability & Statistics, Linear Algebra, Calculus & Optimization, Programming & Data Structures, Database Management & Warehousing

This section carries the highest weightage and covers mathematical and programming foundations. Mastery of probability, linear algebra, and programming is non-negotiable for a good score.

Section 3: Domain — Artificial Intelligence (AI)

Marks: 30 out of 100
Topics: Machine Learning, Artificial Intelligence, Deep Learning, NLP fundamentals

This is the AI-focused section. Topics include search algorithms, logic, probabilistic reasoning, ML algorithms (SVM, decision trees, clustering), neural networks, and NLP basics. This website covers this section in depth.

Recommended Mark Targets:
  • General Aptitude: 12–15 / 15
  • Core Subjects: 35–45 / 55
  • AI Domain: 22–28 / 30
  • Total Target: 70–85 / 100

Time Management Strategies

With 65 questions and 180 minutes, you have an average of 2 minutes 46 seconds per question. However, not all questions are equal in difficulty, so you need a smart allocation strategy.

The Three-Pass Strategy: Scan the entire paper in three deliberate rounds rather than attempting questions sequentially.

Pass 1: Quick Wins (0–60 minutes)

  • Scan all 65 questions quickly
  • Attempt questions you can solve in under 2 minutes
  • Mark doubtful but potentially solvable questions for review
  • Skip any question that looks time-consuming at first glance
  • Target: 25–35 questions answered

Pass 2: Medium Effort (60–135 minutes)

  • Return to marked questions
  • Spend 3–5 minutes per question
  • Use elimination for MCQs to narrow options
  • For NAT, do careful calculations with the virtual calculator
  • Target: 15–20 additional questions

Pass 3: Hard Ones & Review (135–180 minutes)

  • Attempt remaining difficult questions
  • Review all answered questions for silly mistakes
  • Ensure all MSQ and NAT questions are attempted (no penalty!)
  • Double-check bubbling — ensure selected answers are saved

Question Selection Strategy

Not all questions are created equal. A smart question selection strategy can boost your score by 10–15 marks even without additional knowledge.

Priority Matrix:
EasyMediumHard
1-mark MCQAttempt firstPass 2Skip
2-mark MCQAttempt firstPass 2Pass 3 (eliminate)
1-mark MSQ/NATAttempt firstPass 2Guess (no penalty)
2-mark MSQ/NATAttempt firstPass 2Guess (no penalty)
The Expected Value Rule for MCQs: If you can confidently eliminate 2 out of 4 options on a 1-mark MCQ, the expected value of guessing is: 0.5 × (+1) + 0.5 × (−1/3) = +1/3. This is positive, so you should guess! For 2-mark MCQs with 2 eliminated options: 0.5 × (+2) + 0.5 × (−2/3) = +2/3. Always guess when you can eliminate at least two options.

Questions to Avoid

  • MCQs where you cannot eliminate even one option (expected value is negative)
  • Long computation-heavy questions early in the exam (save for Pass 3)
  • Questions from topics you are completely unfamiliar with
  • Questions with lengthy problem statements where understanding itself takes 3+ minutes

Day-Before Preparation

The day before the exam is not for learning new topics. It’s about consolidation, confidence, and logistics.

The 24-Hour Rule: No new topics in the last 24 hours. Only revision of already-studied material.

What to Do

  • Revise the Formula Sheet: Go through all key formulas one final time (see our Master Formula Sheet)
  • Review Common Mistakes: Revisit the Common Mistakes page to avoid known pitfalls
  • Skim your notes: Flip through handwritten or highlighted notes — focus on diagrams and tables
  • Verify logistics: Confirm exam centre address, travel route, and travel time
  • Prepare documents: Print admit card, carry valid photo ID, check for any additional requirements
  • Pack essentials: Admit card, ID, transparent water bottle, simple wristwatch (if allowed)
  • Sleep early: Aim for 7–8 hours of sleep. Your brain consolidates memories during sleep.

What NOT to Do

  • Do not start any new topic or chapter
  • Do not take a full-length mock test (it causes unnecessary fatigue)
  • Do not discuss difficult problems with peers (it creates anxiety)
  • Do not stay up late studying — sleep deprivation hurts performance more than last-minute cramming helps
  • Do not consume excessive caffeine — it can cause anxiety and restlessness

Exam Day Tips

The exam day is about execution, not learning. Follow a calm, practiced routine.

Before the Exam

  • Wake up at least 3 hours before the exam to allow your brain to fully activate
  • Eat a balanced breakfast — protein-rich food provides sustained energy
  • Arrive at the centre 30–45 minutes before reporting time
  • Use the restroom before entering the hall
  • Take slow, deep breaths if you feel anxious — box breathing (4-4-4-4) works well

During the Exam

First 5 Minutes Ritual:
  1. Read the on-screen instructions carefully (you’ll have some mandatory time for this)
  2. Quickly scan all sections to get a feel for the paper
  3. Take a deep breath and start with General Aptitude (usually the easiest section)
  4. Maintain steady pace — don’t rush, don’t linger
  • Use the marking feature: Mark questions for review instead of spending too long on them
  • Don’t panic if you encounter unfamiliar questions — move on and return later
  • Use the virtual calculator for all arithmetic to avoid silly mistakes
  • Track time loosely: Check the clock after every 15–20 questions, not after every question
  • Stay hydrated: Take small sips of water during the exam

Last 15 Minutes

  • Stop attempting new hard questions
  • Review all flagged questions
  • Ensure every MSQ and NAT has an answer (zero penalty for wrong answers)
  • For unanswered MCQs where you cannot eliminate any option, leave them blank
  • Verify that all intended answers are properly saved/submitted

Score Maximization Tactics

Beyond knowledge, these tactical approaches can meaningfully improve your final score.

Tactic 1 — Start with GA: General Aptitude is typically the easiest section. Scoring 12–15 out of 15 in the first 15 minutes builds confidence and momentum.
Tactic 2 — Harvest MSQ/NAT First: Since MSQ and NAT have no negative marking, attempt all of these before returning to tricky MCQs. This maximizes your expected score.
Tactic 3 — Use Dimensional Analysis: For numerical problems, check whether your answer has the right units/dimensions. This catches many errors.
Tactic 4 — Plug-in & Back-solve: For MCQs, sometimes plugging the given options back into the problem is faster than solving from scratch.
Tactic 5 — Elimination by Extremes: When unsure, check boundary or extreme cases. If an option fails at the boundary, it cannot be the general answer.
Score Impact of Attempts:

Suppose you attempt 55 out of 65 questions with 80% accuracy:

  • Correct: ~44 questions → ~66 marks (mix of 1 and 2 markers)
  • Wrong MCQs: ~8 questions → ~−4 marks (average penalty)
  • Wrong MSQ/NAT: ~3 questions → 0 marks penalty
  • Net: ~62 marks — typically a top 2–5% score

Preparation Timeline

Plan your preparation in phases for maximum efficiency.

Phase 1 (Months 1–3): Foundation Building
  • Complete all core mathematics topics (Linear Algebra, Probability, Calculus)
  • Study programming fundamentals and data structures
  • Begin AI chapters 00–07 (Introduction through Reasoning Under Uncertainty)
Phase 2 (Months 4–5): Advanced Topics
  • Complete ML Foundations, Neural Networks, NLP, Computer Vision
  • Study Reinforcement Learning, Planning, and CSP
  • Begin solving topic-wise previous year questions
Phase 3 (Month 6): Revision & Practice
  • Take full-length mock tests (at least 6–8)
  • Review formula sheets and notes weekly
  • Analyse mock test performance and improve weak areas
  • Practice time management under exam conditions
Phase 4 (Last 2 Weeks): Final Sprint
  • Revise only — no new topics
  • Take 2–3 final mock tests under exact exam conditions
  • Review all common mistakes and tricky concepts
  • Focus on mental and physical well-being
