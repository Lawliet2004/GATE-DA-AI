Conditional Probability

GATE Relevance: High

Prerequisites

  • Probability Basics — Sample space, events, axioms

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Define and compute conditional probability P(A|B)
  • Apply the multiplication rule to sequential events
  • Use the chain rule for three or more events
  • Solve urn-style and sequential drawing problems

1. Definition of Conditional Probability

Conditional Probability

The conditional probability of A given B is:

P(A|B) = P(A ∩ B) / P(B),   provided P(B) > 0

Intuitively, once we know B has occurred, the effective sample space shrinks to B, and we ask what fraction of B also includes A.

Example 1 — Fair Die

A fair die is rolled. Given that the outcome is even, what is P(outcome ≥ 5)?

Solution: B = {2, 4, 6} (even), A = {5, 6} (≥5). A ∩ B = {6}.
P(A|B) = P({6}) / P({2,4,6}) = (1/6) / (3/6) = 1/3.

2. Multiplication Rule

Multiplication Rule

Rearranging the conditional probability definition:

P(A ∩ B) = P(B) × P(A|B) = P(A) × P(B|A)

Example 2 — Drawing Cards Sequentially

Two cards are drawn without replacement. P(both Aces)?

Solution: P(1st Ace) = 4/52. P(2nd Ace | 1st Ace) = 3/51.
P(both Aces) = (4/52) × (3/51) = 12/2652 = 1/221 ≈ 0.0045.

3. Chain Rule (Product Rule)

Chain Rule

For events A1, A2, …, An:

P(A1 ∩ A2 ∩ … ∩ An) = P(A1) × P(A2|A1) × P(A3|A1 ∩ A2) × … × P(An|A1 ∩ … ∩ An−1)

Why the Chain Rule Matters

The chain rule is foundational for Bayesian networks, where we decompose a joint distribution into a product of conditional probabilities. We will use this extensively in later sections.

Example 3 — Three Cards

Three cards are drawn without replacement. P(all three are hearts)?

Solution:
P(H1) = 13/52
P(H2|H1) = 12/51
P(H3|H1 ∩ H2) = 11/50
P(all hearts) = (13/52)(12/51)(11/50) = 1716/132600 = 11/850 ≈ 0.0129

4. Urn Problems (Classic GATE Style)

Example 4 — Two-Urn Problem GATE Style

Urn I has 3 red and 4 blue balls. Urn II has 5 red and 2 blue balls. A ball is drawn from Urn I and placed in Urn II, then a ball is drawn from Urn II. What is P(2nd ball is red)?

Solution: Let R1 = red from Urn I, B1 = blue from Urn I, R2 = red from Urn II.

Case 1: Red transferred ⇒ Urn II has 6R, 2B.
P(R1) × P(R2|R1) = (3/7)(6/8) = 18/56

Case 2: Blue transferred ⇒ Urn II has 5R, 3B.
P(B1) × P(R2|B1) = (4/7)(5/8) = 20/56

P(R2) = 18/56 + 20/56 = 38/56 = 19/28 ≈ 0.6786

Example 5 — Sequential Without Replacement GATE Style NAT

A bag has 5 white and 3 black balls. Two balls are drawn without replacement. P(second ball is white)?

Solution:
P(W2) = P(W1)P(W2|W1) + P(B1)P(W2|B1)
= (5/8)(4/7) + (3/8)(5/7) = 20/56 + 15/56 = 35/56 = 5/8 = 0.625

Insight: Marginal probability of the second draw equals the marginal probability of the first draw when drawing without replacement from a homogeneous pool!

Summary

  • Conditional probability: P(A|B) = P(A ∩ B) / P(B)
  • Multiplication rule: P(A ∩ B) = P(A)P(B|A)
  • Chain rule extends multiplication to n events — the basis for Bayesian network factorization
  • For urn/sequential problems, enumerate cases and use the law of total probability
