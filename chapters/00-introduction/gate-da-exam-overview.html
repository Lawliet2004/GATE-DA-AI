GATE DA Exam Overview

GATE Relevance: High

Prerequisites

  • AI vs ML vs DL (recommended)

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Understand the GATE DA exam structure, duration, and marks distribution
  • Know the AI-related topics in the GATE DA syllabus
  • Learn the question types and marking scheme
  • Develop an effective preparation strategy for the AI section

1. What is GATE DA?

GATE (Graduate Aptitude Test in Engineering) is a national-level examination conducted jointly by IISc Bangalore and seven IITs. It tests comprehensive understanding of various undergraduate and postgraduate subjects in engineering and science.

GATE DA (Data Science and Artificial Intelligence) is a relatively new paper introduced to address the growing demand for data science and AI professionals. It covers mathematics, statistics, programming, data science, machine learning, and artificial intelligence.

Why GATE DA?

GATE DA scores are used for admission to M.Tech / MS / PhD programs in Data Science and AI at IITs, IISc, NITs, and other premier institutions. Some PSUs and private companies also accept GATE scores for recruitment. A good GATE DA score opens doors to the finest research and academic institutions in India.

2. Exam Pattern

Parameter Details
Duration 3 hours (180 minutes)
Total Questions 65 questions
Total Marks 100 marks
Question Types MCQ (Multiple Choice), MSQ (Multiple Select), NAT (Numerical Answer Type)
Marks per Question 1 mark or 2 marks
Negative Marking (MCQ) 1-mark Q: −1/3 | 2-mark Q: −2/3
Negative Marking (MSQ/NAT) No negative marking
Mode Online (Computer Based Test)
Important: Negative Marking Strategy

For MCQs, there is negative marking (−1/3 for 1-mark, −2/3 for 2-mark). This means blind guessing is statistically unfavorable. However, if you can eliminate 2 out of 4 options, guessing becomes favorable. For MSQ and NAT questions, there is no negative marking, so always attempt these even if you are unsure.

High-Priority AI Topics for GATE DA

Based on analysis of past GATE DA papers, the following AI topics appear most frequently and carry the most marks:

  1. Search Algorithms (BFS, DFS, A*, Minimax, Alpha-Beta) — at least 2–3 questions every year
  2. Propositional & Predicate Logic (Inference, Resolution, Unification) — 1–2 questions
  3. Probabilistic Reasoning (Bayes’ theorem, Bayesian Networks) — 1–2 questions
  4. Agent types and PEAS — occasional 1-mark questions

5. Question Types in Detail

5.1 MCQ (Multiple Choice Questions)

Four options, exactly one correct. Negative marking applies. These test conceptual understanding and the ability to quickly identify the right answer.

5.2 MSQ (Multiple Select Questions)

Four options, one or more may be correct. You get full marks only if you select ALL correct options and NO incorrect ones. No partial marking. No negative marking. These test deeper understanding.

5.3 NAT (Numerical Answer Type)

You type a numerical value. No options are provided. No negative marking. A range of acceptable answers is defined (e.g., 3.98 to 4.02). These test computational/problem-solving skills.

Example: AI-Related GATE DA Question Types

MCQ example: “In A* search, if the heuristic function h(n) is admissible, the algorithm is guaranteed to be: (a) Complete (b) Optimal (c) Both complete and optimal (d) Neither.”

NAT example: “In a game tree with branching factor 3 and depth 4, how many leaf nodes does Minimax evaluate without pruning?” (Answer: 81 = 34)

MSQ example: “Which of the following search algorithms are complete when the state space is finite? (Select all that apply) (a) DFS (b) BFS (c) DLS (d) IDDFS” (Answer: a, b, d)

6. Preparation Strategy for the AI Section

Strategy 1: Master the Fundamentals First

Before diving into advanced topics, make sure you have a rock-solid foundation in search algorithms and logic. These two areas account for the majority of AI questions in GATE DA. Start with BFS/DFS, then move to A*, then Minimax/Alpha-Beta.

Strategy 2: Practice Numerical Problems

GATE favors NAT-type questions where you trace through algorithms by hand. Practice:

  • Tracing BFS/DFS on graphs and noting the order of node expansion
  • Computing f(n) = g(n) + h(n) for A* search step by step
  • Building Minimax trees and applying alpha-beta pruning
  • Resolving clauses in propositional and predicate logic
  • Computing posterior probabilities using Bayes’ theorem
Strategy 3: Use the Right Textbook

The standard reference for GATE DA AI is Russell & Norvig: “Artificial Intelligence: A Modern Approach” (AIMA). Chapters 1–7, 13, 18–19 of AIMA directly map to the GATE DA AI syllabus. This website follows the same structure and content.

Strategy 4: Solve Previous Year Papers

Since GATE DA is relatively new, also practice AI questions from GATE CS (Computer Science) papers, which have tested these same topics for decades. The AI section of GATE CS is very similar to GATE DA’s AI portion.

Strategy 5: Time Management

You have 180 minutes for 65 questions. That’s approximately 2.77 minutes per question. For the AI section (~8–10 questions), budget about 25 minutes. Practice solving AI problems within time constraints.

Recommended Study Order

  1. Intelligent Agents (quick overview) — 1 day
  2. Uninformed Search (thorough) — 3–4 days
  3. Informed Search (thorough) — 3–4 days
  4. Adversarial Search (thorough) — 3–4 days
  5. Propositional Logic (thorough) — 3–4 days
  6. Predicate Logic (thorough) — 3–4 days
  7. Probabilistic Reasoning (moderate) — 3 days
  8. ML & Neural Networks (covered in separate sections) — ongoing
  9. Revision & past papers — 3–5 days

Total estimated time for AI section: 20–25 days of focused study.

7. Quick Reference: Key AI Facts for GATE

Fact Detail
BFS Time Complexity O(bd) where b = branching factor, d = depth
DFS Time Complexity O(bm) where m = max depth
A* Optimality Condition h(n) must be admissible (never overestimates)
A* on Graph: Optimality Requires consistency (h(n) ≤ c(n,n′) + h(n′))
Minimax Value MAX nodes: max of children; MIN nodes: min of children
Alpha-Beta Best Case Examines O(bd/2) nodes (with perfect move ordering)
Modus Ponens From P and P → Q, conclude Q
Resolution From (A ∨ B) and (¬B ∨ C), conclude (A ∨ C)
Bayes’ Theorem P(A|B) = P(B|A) · P(A) / P(B)

📚 Summary

  • GATE DA is a 3-hour, 65-question, 100-mark online exam.
  • Question types: MCQ (negative marking), MSQ (no negative), NAT (no negative).
  • The AI section carries approximately 15 marks with 8–10 questions.
  • High-priority topics: Search (uninformed + informed + adversarial), Logic (propositional + predicate), Probabilistic reasoning.
  • Primary reference: Russell & Norvig, AIMA.
  • Practice previous GATE CS AI questions + GATE DA papers.
  • Budget ~25 days for thorough AI preparation.
